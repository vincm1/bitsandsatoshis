# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

DACH region, 30–50 years old. Curious about Bitcoin but exhausted by the noise — the hype cycles, the Twitter screaming, the "10x your money" promises. They read newsletters like they read a good magazine: unhurried, on their own terms. They are not traders. They want to *understand*, and they want someone who respects their intelligence. They subscribe because they trust the voice, not because they need the latest price action.

## Product Purpose

Bits&Satoshis is a weekly German-language Bitcoin newsletter. One email, five minutes, no hype. The website exists to convert curious visitors into subscribers and to build a sense of belonging — this is *their* calm corner of Bitcoin. Secondary goal: conversion into the Bitcoin AI learning app.

## Brand Personality

Klar. Mutig. Warm.

The closest physical analogy: Arnold's Pump Club adapted for Bitcoin — a newsletter with genuine personality and visual confidence, built around community identity rather than content delivery. Not a newspaper. Not a financial product. A place people feel they belong to.

The tone is quiet confidence: strong opinions, held calmly. Never shouting. Never hedging into blandness. The brand takes up space without being loud.

## Reference

**Arnold's Pump Club** (arnoldspumpclub.com): warm paper foundation, ultra-bold Chaney display type filling the viewport, cut-out B&W photography with bold colored-border framing, community-first framing, "positive corner of the internet" energy. Fonts: **Chaney** (headings) + **Satoshi Variable** (body). The confidence to commit to a strong aesthetic rather than default to safe editorial restraint.

What to take: the warm paper base + vivid accent contrast, the sense that this is *someone's* thing (not a corporate product), the cut-out illustration-friendly space, the ultra-bold typographic decisions, the physical-collage energy.

What to adapt: swap the gym energy for focused calm. The confidence stays; the intensity drops. Bitcoin Orange replaces crimson. German editorial seriousness replaces American hype — but with visual boldness, not newspaper conservatism. Drop the comic-book lightning bolts; keep the physicality and bold type.

## Anti-references

- **Crypto trading platforms** (CoinMarketCap, Binance): charts, tickers, green/red price deltas, rocket emoji, dashboard density. The visual language of speculation.
- **Generic SaaS landing pages**: floating cards, metric blocks, gradient text, a CTA every 200px, "Used by 10,000 teams" social proof clusters.
- **VC fintech** (Revolut, N26): navy + gold, corporate sans, trust-signaling badge arrays, "the future of money" platitudes.
- **Influencer finance content**: YouTube-thumbnail energy, shocking colors, bold unsubstantiated claims, countdown timers.
- **Identity-less editorial cream**: warm-restrained, beige-gray, forgettably competent — what happens when the paper palette is used without typographic conviction or personality. The warmth of the background means nothing if everything on top is timid.

## Design Principles

1. **Paper is a material, not a default.** The `#F4EDE0` background isn't warm-cream-by-habit. It's newsprint — the specific warmth of the physical object the brand's editorial identity runs on. Bitcoin Orange on newsprint hits like a pull-quote stamp on a real newsletter. The warmth earns its place through the physical analogy; what kills it is timidity on top. Bold Chaney headlines at 70px+ on paper are what the reference looks like. Own the material completely.
2. **Personality over polish.** A slightly rough illustration edge beats a sterile icon. A strong serif headline beats a safe geometric weight. Craft is in the conviction of choices, not in how many corners got rounded.
3. **Orange earns its place.** Bitcoin Orange is used for one thing: the thing that matters most on this screen. Not decoration, not borders, not backgrounds. It marks what to act on.
4. **Community, not content.** Every design decision should reinforce "I belong here" over "I am reading content." Social proof, consistent voice, warmth in the empty states and CTAs.
5. **Calm is not timid.** Quietness is expressed through spacing, pace, and restraint in copy — not through gray-washing the palette or shrinking the type. The design can be visually bold and tonally calm at the same time.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Minimum 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large/bold headings. Warm paper backgrounds require care on secondary text (`--c-stone` on `--c-paper`) — the warm tint reduces effective contrast subtly; test all muted text, never assume. Reduced-motion support for all animations. Keyboard navigation for the signup form and nav.
